Theory-Discrete Fourier Transform

 

A transform is essentially a mathematical mapping process. When it is used in image analysis and processing, it provides insight into the manner in which the gray levels change within an image – the spatial frequency content of an image, the orientation, and so on. The Fourier transform is a well known example of a transform. Another popular transform, the principal component transform decorrelates multiband image data so that the redundancy in the dataset can be minimized.

A transform maps image data into a different mathematical space via a transformation equation, such as the Fourier transform equation. Most of the transforms map the image data from the spatial domain to the frequency domain (also called the spectral domain), where all the pixels in the input (spatial domain) contribute to each value in the output (frequency domain), and vice versa. A pixel in the spatial domain receives contribution from all the frequencies in the frequency domain. The various terms used here will be explained below.

 

Illustration of mapping between spatial and transform domains

In the field of digital image processing, these transforms are used in their discrete (sampled) form. The discrete version is created by sampling the continuous form of the functions on which these transforms are based, that is, the basis functions. The discrete version of the basis functions is known as the basis vectors.

Basis images or basis matrices are two-dimensional (2-D) versions of basis vectors. The process of transforming the image data into another domain, or mathematical space, amounts to projecting the image onto the basis images. The mathematical term for this projection process is an inner product or scalar product. Frequency transforms can be performed on the entire image or smaller blocks (as in case of discrete cosine transform for JPEG image compression).

Spatial frequency relates to how brightness levels change relative to spatial coordinates, in other words, spatial frequency relates to the rate of change of bright levels per unit distance. It is convention to use the term frequency for sinusoidal transforms, and sequence for rectangular wave transforms. Rapidly changing brightness values over short distances correspond to high frequency terms, and slowly changing brightness values correspond to low frequency terms.

The indices are changed to (m,n) to avoid the confusion between this “j” and the j in pixel location (i,j).  m and n vary from 0 to N-1, and thus for each pair of (u,v) we get N x N values of these complex exponential terms.

 Fourier Transform

 Fourier transform decomposes a complex signal into a weighted sum of a zero frequency term (the DC term which is related to the average value), and sinusoidal terms, the basis functions, where each sinusoid is a harmonic of the fundamental. The fundamental  is the basic or lowest frequency. For instance, if the fundamental is 1 cycle per millimeter, then its harmonics are 2 cycles/mm, 3 cycles/mm, and so on.

 From the transform, the original signal can be recreated by adding the fundamental and all the harmonics, with each term weighted by its corresponding transform coefficient.

Properties of the 2-D Fourier Transform

 

Separability

 

This is one of important properties of the Fourier transform. This implies that the two-dimensional basis image can be decomposed into two product terms where each term depends only on the rows or columns. Therefore the 2-D DFT can be found by successive application of two 1-D DFTs. This is evident from the equation of the 2-dimensional Fourier transform. For each row, the one-dimensional DFT is first computed, and stored in an array. On this array, the one-dimensional DFT is then computed, but the order is along the columns. In this process, the computations are considerably simplified, and the complexity of both the hardware and software implementations becomes less. Efficient computations of the DFT are known under a famous abbreviation FFT, or Fast Fourier Transform. Very efficient algorithms exist when the image is square, whose rows and columns are an integral power of 2, i.e., M = N = 2p, where P is an integer. Research based on the mathematical theory of numbers produced algorithms for non-square size images, whose rows and columns may even be prime numbers.

 

Linearity

 The Fourier transform is a linear operator and is shown by the fol

 Let g(m,n) = a.f1(m,n) + b.f2(m,n)

 Then it can be shown that G(u,v) = a.F1(u,v) + b.F2(u,v)

 where a and b are constants. Similar relation applies to the inverse Fourier transform as well.

 

Convolution

Convolution in one domain is the equivalent of multiplication in the other domain. This is what allows us to perform filtering in the spatial domain with convolution masks. We use * to denote the convolution operation, and F[ ] for the forward Fourier transform and similarly, F-1[ ] for the inverse Fourier transform.  

The equations that define convolution  property are:

 F[f(m,n) * h(m,n)]   =   F(u,v) . H(u,v)

 F[F(u,v) * H(u,v)] = f(m,n) . h(m,n)

It may be computationally less intensive to apply filters in the spatial domain of the image rather than the frequency domain of the image, especially if parallel hardware is available.

  

Translation

 

The translation property of the Fourier transform is given by the following equations:

 

F[f(m – mo, n – no]  =  F(u,v). exp[-j2p{(mou/M) + (nov/N)}]

 

These equations tell us that if the image is moved, the resulting Fourier spectrum undergoes a phase shift, but magnitude of the spectrum remains the same. This is evident from the fact that the magnitude of the complex exponential is equal to 1.

 Similar relation applies to the inverse Fourier transform as well:

 F-1[F(u – uo, v – vo]  =  f(m,n). exp[+j2p{(uom/M) + (vov/N)}]

 This is also known as the  modulation property, or the frequency translation property.

 These equations tell us that if the image is multiplied by a complex exponential (sinusoid), its corresponding spectrum is shifted. This is the principle in communication systems where, for instance, low frequency speech signal is carried on a high frequency carrier signal.

 

Rotation

 This property tell us that if an image is rotated by an angle θ0, then F(u, v) is rotated by the same angle, and vice versa. The rotation property can be easily illustrated by using polar coordinates.

 

Displaying the Fourier Spectrum

The Fourier spectrum consists of complex floating point numbers, stored with a data format of complex – where the image structure contains a matrix structure with one matrix for the real part and one for the imaginary part. We shift the origin to the center of the image by applying the properties of periodicity and modulation with u0 = v0 = N/2 which is equivalent to multiplying the image with (-1)m+n, for every pixel at location (m,n). This makes it convenient to display the Fourier magnitude spectrum with the zero frequency at the centre, with frequency increasing in radial direction, away from the centre.

 

This helps visualize the concepts of low and high frequencies better, and most software packages provide interactive selection of cut-off points for low and highpass filtering based on this form of display of the magnitude spectrum. An example is shown below:

                        

 

To take advantage of the human visual system’s response to brightness we can greatly enhance the visual information available by displaying the following log transform of the spectrum:

 

L(u,v)   =    K.[1 + |Log{|F(u,v)|}

 

The log function compresses the data, and the scaling factor K remaps the data to the 0-255 range.

 

Phase

 

The phase spectrum is displayed primarily to illustrate phase changes. It has a range of 0 to 360 degrees, or 0 to 2π radians. The phase data are stored in floating point data, so it has a larger dynamic range than the 256 levels typically

available for display. The data are rescaled to 0-255 range for on-screen display purposes.
